\section*{Notations and formulas}

\subsection*{Contribution to inflation}
\label{sec:app-contributions}

The contribution of product $j$ to inflation for household group $g$ is
computed using the product price movement and the group-specific product
weight. Because the price index is chained annually, the contribution in month
$m$ of year $y$ uses the relevant current and previous HICP weight years:

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\text{Contrib}_{j,g,y,m}
=
\frac{I_{g,y-1,12}}{I_{g,y-1,m}}
\times
\omega_{j,g,y-1}
\times
\left(
\frac{p_{j,y,m} - p_{j,y-1,12}}{p_{j,y-1,12}}
\right)
+ \\
\frac{I_{g,y-2,12}}{I_{g,y-1,m}}
\times
\omega_{j,g,y-2}
\times
\left(
\frac{p_{j,y-1,12} - p_{j,y-1,m}}{p_{j,y-2,12}}
\right).
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $p_{j,y,m}$ is the price index of product $j$ in month $m$ of year $y$,
$I_{g,y,m}$ is the chained price index for group $g$, and
$\omega_{j,g,y}$ is the group-specific HICP weight of product $j$ in weight
year $y$.

This approach keeps product-level contributions consistent with the chained
inflation measures used for each household group. It is therefore useful for
interpreting which product categories drive the inflation rate and the
inflation gap between groups.



A simpler alternative starts from the unnormalized HBS-adjusted weight

\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\omega}_{j,g,y}
=
\omega^{HICP}_{j,y}
\cdot
c^{HBS}_{j,g,y'}.
\end{equation}

Because the correction factor is a product-by-product ratio, these adjusted
weights do not necessarily sum to one within each household group. The
within-group normalized weight is therefore

\begin{equation}
\omega^{norm}_{j,g,y}
=
\frac{
\omega^{HICP}_{j,y}
\cdot
c^{HBS}_{j,g,y'}
}{
\sum_{k \in \mathcal{J}_y}
\omega^{HICP}_{k,y}
\cdot
c^{HBS}_{k,g,y'}
},
\end{equation}

where $y'$ is the HBS wave matched to HICP weight year $y$. This normalization gives each household group a complete basket, so
differences in inflation across groups come from differences in basket
composition rather than from differences in total basket size. However, it does
not impose that the population-weighted average of group-specific item weights
matches the official HICP item weights. For this reason, the baseline
specification uses the RAS calibration described below.

This within-group normalization can be interpreted as a special case in which
household groups are given equal aggregate weights, denoted $a_g=1/n$ for $n$
groups, rather than using the observed consumption share $s_g$ of each group.
Equivalently, the recalculated aggregate index is obtained as a simple average
of the group-specific indices. This relative
expenditure approach is often used in empirical applications because it only
requires group-specific HBS expenditure profiles and aggregate price indices;
for example, it is close to the strategy used in the Bruegel inflation
inequality updates for EU households \citep{claeys2022inflation}.

\subsection*{Inflation and inflation gap}


Inflation in a given month for each household group $g$ results from the sum
of product-level contributions, defined in
Appendix~\ref{sec:app-contributions}:

\begin{equation} 
\pi_{g,y,m}
=
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{J}_y}
\text{Contrib}_{j,g,y,m}.
\end{equation} 


The inflation gap between two household groups $g_1$ and $g_2$ is defined as:

\begin{equation} 
\Delta\pi_{g_1,g_2,y,m}
=
\pi_{g_1,y,m}
-
\pi_{g_2,y,m}.
\end{equation}

For income quintiles, the inter-quintile inflation gap is:

\begin{equation} 
\text{IQR}_{y,m}
=
\pi_{Q1,y,m}
-
\pi_{Q5,y,m}.
\end{equation} \\

Scanner data capture price changes at the barcode level, allowing detailed
analysis of inflation within specific product categories (e.g., organic vs.
regular products). For instance, \cite{jaravel2024distributional} shows that
the inflation gap calculated using scanner data for organic versus conventional
products in the United States was nearly double that observed using aggregated
CPI data. \citet{jaravel2019unequal} also shows that lower-income households
benefited less from product innovation and faced different effective price
changes across detailed retail products. Our approach differs in three ways: we
use Spanish HBS/EPF expenditure records rather than scanner data, we match those
baskets to aggregate HICP price indices rather than household-specific
transaction prices, and we keep a fixed 2020 household basket rather than
tracking product substitution within households. The exercise therefore does not
reproduce scanner-data granularity, but it moves one step closer to the
household level by replacing group-average expenditure shares with
household-specific baskets.

\section{Using Scanner data to Mesure Inflation Inequality}

Our approach can only capture the part of inflation inequality that appears between household categories (e.g., first and fifth quintiles). Recent advances in data availability, such as scanner data\footnote{ Scanner data are detailed data is available in supermarkets from the recording of the transaction. Private companies such as Nielsen is the most famous make available this kind of data.} from supermarkets and bank client data, allow for more granular insights into inflation experiences. Accurate measurement requires granular price and spending data due to aggregation bias.

\cite{jaravel2024distributional} investigated inflation inequality in the United States from 2004 to 2023, highlighting a significant aggregation bias. Using  the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and Consumer Expenditure Survey (CEX) data, the calculated inflation gap is 0.37 percentage points. However, using scanner data revealed a considerably larger gap of 0.66 percentage points, with the first quintile  experiencing 1.87\% inflation and the fifth quintile experiencing 1.21\% inflation.

\section{The `inflationinequality`  package}

All figures of the paper are plotted with the package `inflationinequality`. This package provides tools to measure inflation inequality. Its core functionalities include the calculation and visualization of inflation rates and contributions to inflation by household categories, as well as the simulation of counterfactual scenarios for unconventional price policies. 

By default, the package retrieves datasets with the highest granularity of COICOP levels, specifically level 2. However, users can opt to download only COICOP level 1 data. Through integration with DBnomics\footnote{To construct these series we use data from DBnomics. The DBnomics API is called using the rdbnomics package.}, the package facilitates the retrieval and the updateof Consumer Price Index (CPI) and Household Budget Survey (HBS) data from any euro area country. This allows for cross-country comparisons, leveraging the standardized definitions of categories across the  euro area. 

The inflationinequality package use  objects to manage metadata associated with its datasets efficiently. For example, the object of class "inflation" encapsulates actual inflation data alongside key metadata, including categories (an ordered list of demographic classifications).Associated Vignettes describe more in detail the features of the `inflationinequality` package.  

The package also includes  plotting functions, such as time series visualizations and grouped bar charts, enabling users to analyze contributions to inflation and compare inflation rates across countries for specific years. 


\section{Impact of Monetary Policy on Inflation Inequalities}

Faced with the recent inflationary shock, monetary policy has been tightened in the euro area since July 2022. The rate-tightening cycle, combined with the decline in wholesale gas and electricity prices, has contributed to the slowdown in inflation since mid-2023.

While inflation has clear distributional effects, monetary policy also generates distributional consequences (\cite{ampudia2024shopping}). Rate hikes can increase income from savings but also raise debt costs, disproportionately affecting lower-income households. Direct effects occur through changes in income from savings and loan interest (rate increases benefit wealth holders). Indirect effects are transmitted via the labor market, where higher rates can depress job creation and increase inequality through job losses (Hubert and Savignac, 2024).
