\section{Upper-Level Substitution Bias}
\label{app:substitution-bias}

Fixed-basket indices can overstate the cost of living when households substitute
away from products whose relative prices increase. Our baseline follows a
Laspeyres-type HICP aggregation, because this is the convention needed to stay
close to official consumer price indices. As a simple diagnostic, this appendix
compares the baseline aggregation with a Tornqvist alternative for France in
2020, using the same COICOP level-3 price data and income-quintile expenditure
weights.

The exercise is deliberately narrow. For each group, we compute the annual
growth rate directly between January and December 2020 under the two aggregation
formulas. The substitution-bias diagnostic is the Laspeyres annual growth rate
minus the Tornqvist annual growth rate, in percentage points. A positive value
means that the fixed-weight aggregation grows faster than the Tornqvist
alternative over the same year.

The estimates are small for all groups. In this example, switching from the
baseline Laspeyres-type aggregation to the Tornqvist alternative changes annual
inflation by less than one tenth of a percentage point for each income quintile.
This suggests that upper-level substitution is not a major driver of the
income-gradient in measured inflation in this year.
