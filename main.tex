\begin{abstract}
This article examines the heterogeneous effects of inflation across household categories in the euro area. Differences in consumption patterns between households and inflation dispersion across products have generated inflation inequalities. The inflationary episode, driven by rising imported prices, has mainly affected households who spend a larger share of their income on energy and food. In large countries of the euro area, inflation differentials between income quintiles have been low over the period, but differentials by age and residence area have been more pronounced. The tariff shields have significantly contributed to reducing inflation inequalities.


\vspace{1em} 

\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Inflation, Distribution of Income, Unconventional Fiscal Policy\\
\noindent \textbf{JEL codes:} E31, D30, C43 

\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}


This study examines the effect of the recent inflation surge on euro area households. Since mid-2021, inflation rates in the euro area rose sharply, displaying considerable dispersion across countries and socio-demographic groups. High inflation disproportionately impacts households with differing consumption patterns, such as higher spending on energy and food. 

%The consumer price index (CPI) typically reflects an average rate of price change for an average household. However, households consume different baskets of goods, leading to wide variations around the average. The main objective of this paper is to document how households face heterogeneous inflation because of the differences in their consumption patterns, and to measure the effects of unconventional fiscal policies decided in 2022-2023.


The literature identifies three channels through which inflation produces heterogeneous effects: wealth, income, and consumption. Inflation negatively impacts wealth by eroding the real value of nominal assets. Conversely, it benefits indebted households, particularly those with fixed interest rate loans, as the real value of their debt diminishes. Moreover, inflation reduces the purchasing power of income, especially for households with incomes and savings not fully adjusted to inflation. Base wages, which often do not fully adjust to inflation, remain unchanged for the typical one-year wage contract period. Recent studies, such as \cite{cardoso2022heterogeneous} and \cite{pallotti2024bears}, have sought to estimate these three channels simultaneously. Their analysis leverages newly available granular bank account data, which offer detailed insights into household financial positions and variations in consumption structures.

This work builds on studies by \cite{bobasu2023impact} and \cite{claeys2022inflation} that analyzed inflation inequality by household category, with slightly differing methodologies and results. \cite{bobasu2023impact} estimates inter-quintile inflation gaps of approximately 0.8 percentage points in September 2022, which narrowed to zero by early 2023 for the euro area, using 2015 household budget survey weights. The Bruegel Institute has published and updated inflation rates by income quintile for European Union countries. Their findings align with our results. However, the methodology exclusively uses HBS weights, without adjusting the weights based on HBS-corrected price indices for quintile-specific inflation (Q1 and Q5). This adjustment leads to slight discrepancies between their overall inflation estimates and the harmonized price indices of the respective countries. Using individual data from the Family Budget survey, OFCE (2022) calculates a specific inflation rate for each household in France. The study stresses the fact that the differences between income deciles are small, but much greater within each decile, where age and area of residence explain the dispersion. Thus, in the first decile, 25\% of households have an average inflation of 3.9\%, but another quarter have an inflation of 6.8\%, with smaller intra-decile differences as income rises. 

Scanner data capture price changes at the barcode level, allowing detailed analysis of inflation within specific product categories (e.g., organic vs. regular products). For instance, \cite{jaravel2024distributional} shows that the inflation gap calculated using scanner data for organic versus conventional
products in the United States was nearly double that observed using aggregated CPI data.



We calculate inflation rates experienced by households across different categories within each euro area country, as well as for the euro area as a whole. Inflation rates by categories are first computed at the country level and then aggregated for the euro area following the HICP methodology. 

This paper makes two contributions to the renewed interest in inflation inequality. First, it provides a publicly available and easily updatable dataset constructed using harmonized country-level inflation rates by household categories, ensuring consistency with the official Harmonized Consumer Price Index. Second, it examines the effect of unconventional fiscal policies, with a particular focus on the mitigating impacts of tariff shield policies and temporary VAT cuts implemented across euro area countries. The study also underscores disparities in fiscal policy outcomes, highlighting the success of energy-focused measures, such as VAT reductions.

%This paper contributes to the understanding of inflation inequality by constructing publicly available and easily updatable inflation indicators for various household categories in the euro area. These indicators are based on harmonized country-level inflation data and are consistent with the official Harmonised Consumer Price Index (HICP). Additionally, the study assesses the effect of unconventional fiscal policies implemented in 2022-2023, highlighting the mitigating effects of tariff shield policies and temporary VAT cuts across euro area countries.

Our main results are the following. Inflation inequality exhibits substantial variation across euro area countries. Within large economies, such as Germany and France, inflation inequality remains relatively low since 2021. An exception is Italy, where disparities became pronounced during the energy price surge. In large countries, the inflation gap between the first and the fifth income quintiles is negligible. However, more significant differences emerge when considering demographic factors such as age and geographical factors, particularly between urban and rural households.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. The first section describes the data and the methodology. The second section examines the developments of inflation inequality in the euro area. The third section presents simulation exercises, including input-output price shocks and the effect of unconventional fiscal policies in France and Spain.

\section{Data and Methodology}

This section introduces the data sources and methodological framework employed in the analysis to build the inflation inequality indicators.
% Moreover, we provide an open-access package that enables users to replicate and update our indicators.

\subsection{Data}

%The aim of calculating inflation  by household category is to construct inflation  that takes account of differences in household consumption structure. The data includes HICP by country, HBS weights (with a discussion on harmonization), and country weights based on country consumption.


Headline inflation represents the spending patterns of an average household. Estimating inflation for specific household categories requires the use of Household Budget Surveys (HBS), which are national surveys on consumer expenditure. These surveys offer detailed insights into household characteristics, including income levels, the age of the household reference person, and the area of residence.

\subsection*{The Harmonized Index of Consumer Prices}

This analysis is based on publicly available data, primarily the Harmonized Index of Consumer Prices (HICP) constructed by national statistical institutes in collaboration with Eurostat. 

The HICP offers a standardized measure of inflation, ensuring comparability among member states with 2015 as the base year. Eurostat aggregates national HICPs. Each country's HICP is based on price changes for a fixed basket of goods and services, weighted according to household expenditure from country-specific sources (national accounts, HBS, etc.). These indices are then combined into the aggregate HICP using country-specific weights that reflect their share of total EU household consumption expenditure. The calculation ensures comparability by adhering to EU regulations and consistent data collection practices.

To ensure the HICP accurately represents current consumption patterns, Eurostat updates its weighting structure annually. The updated weights are implemented with the January release of the HICP each year and remain fixed throughout the calendar year. This practice ensures consistency in the measurement of price changes over the year. This approach aligns with the Laspeyres index formula, which uses fixed base-period weights to measure price changes over time. 

Figure 1 illustrates the developments across different HICP components in the euro area since 2014. The HICP components show significant volatility in energy and fuel prices. The sharp rise starting around mid-2021, peaking in 2022, is due to post-COVID recovery, supply chain disruptions, and geopolitical tensions. After the peaks in 2022, there is a visible correction across most components. Energy prices dropped sharply, while food prices and other components showed more gradual stabilization.

\subsection*{Household Budget Survey} 


The heterogeneity of consumption patterns among households is a well-documented pattern (e.g. "Engel's laws"). However, the accurate measurement of household-level consumption continues to present significant challenges. 

The Household Budget Surveys (HBS) are conducted on a large sample of households in each euro area country. Households are required to document their expenditures on consumer goods and services over a designated period, typically two weeks. These surveys capture the values of expenditures across COICOP categories.

HBS data are generally collected every five years. The most recent HBS wave generally took place in 2020. The 2020 HBS wave was actually conducted between 2018 and 2022 in most EU Member States, while data for Cyprus, Malta, and France were gathered earlier, between 2015 and 2017.\footnote{The following HBS wave is the 2025 wave, with increased harmonization between countries.}

This infrequent collection may not fully capture short-term fluctuations in consumption patterns, particularly during periods of rapid price changes (e.g. COVID-19), potentially leading to bias in inflation inequality during such periods.\footnote{\cite{accardo2023measuring} developed a methodology to address the issue of low-frequency data in household consumption surveys.} Significant changes in weights between successive waves of HBS can also result in abrupt variations in inflation estimates by category. To address this, a linear interpolation method is proposed to smooth variations.\footnote{To mitigate this issue, two approaches are available in the package: linearly interpolate the HBS weights to smooth out the changes in weights over time, or apply a specific year's HBS weights for all months when calculating inflation rates (or contributions to inflation).}



Figure 2 presents the consumption baskets by income quintiles, revealing the disparities in expenditure patterns across different income groups. Notably, lower-income households allocate a larger proportion of their expenditure to essential goods such as food and energy, making them more vulnerable to price shocks in these categories.



Depending on the level of data available, HICP and HBS are merged at the lowest COICOP level possible in each country. In certain cases, such as for France's Insee or Italy's Istat, data published directly by national statistical institutes can be utilized instead of relying on the Eurostat dataset. This approach is particularly valuable when the nationally published data provide greater granularity, recency, or frequency. \footnote{The companion R package allows the use of custom data.}
HBS contains many variables according to which consumption can be broken down. To study the heterogeneous effects of inflation, we use income, age and residence area. Moreover, Eurostat provides HBS with breakdowns at 2-digit COICOP. At level 2 of the COICOP, the merge is bijective. In line with the Eurostat HICP methodology, imputed rent is excluded from the analysis. This analysis uses all available waves of the HBS since 1999 and matches them with the closest corresponding HICP year.

\subsection{Methodology}

%We explain the harmonized methodology applied across all euro area countries, utilizing an R package. The average of inflation measures by categories equals average inflation (cf. Jaravel?). The package allows to use custom data . For the French case, we reference the annual inflation data from INSEE. 


\subsection*{Weights}

Let $\omega^{HICP}_{j,y}$ denote the official annual HICP weight of product
category $j$ in weight year $y$. Let $g$ denote a household group, such as an
income quintile, an age group, or a residence-area group. The HBS correction
factor is defined as:

\begin{equation}
c^{HBS}_{j,g,y'}
=
\frac{
E^{HBS}_{j,g,y'}
}{
E^{HBS}_{j,\mathrm{all},y'}
},
\end{equation}

where $E^{HBS}_{j,g,y'}$ is HBS expenditure on product category $j$ by group
$g$ in HBS wave $y'$, and $E^{HBS}_{j,\mathrm{all},y'}$ is the corresponding
expenditure for all households. The group-specific product weight is then:

\begin{equation}
\omega_{j,g,y}
=
\frac{
\omega^{HICP}_{j,y}
\cdot
c^{HBS}_{j,g,y'}
}{
\sum_{k \in \mathcal{J}_y}
\omega^{HICP}_{k,y}
\cdot
c^{HBS}_{k,g,y'_k}
},
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{J}_y$ is the set of product categories included in the HICP
weighting structure in year $y$, and $y'_k$ is the HBS wave matched to product
category $k$ and HICP weight year $y$.

The HBS correction alone gives adjusted, unnormalized weights:

\begin{equation}
\widetilde{\omega}_{j,g,y}
=
\omega^{HICP}_{j,y}
\cdot
c^{HBS}_{j,g,y'}.
\end{equation}

These adjusted weights do not necessarily sum to one, because the correction
factor is a product-by-product ratio. It indicates whether group $g$ spends
relatively more or less on product $j$ than all households, but the positive
and negative deviations across products are not constrained to offset exactly.
The denominator is therefore the normalization term. It rescales the adjusted
weights so that each household group is assigned a complete basket whose
product weights sum to one. Thus, differences in inflation across household groups come from differences
in the composition of the basket across products, not from differences in the
total size of the basket.

The Household Budget Survey is conducted only once every five years. In
contrast, the expenditure weights used in the construction of the HICP are
updated annually. The year of the HBS data $y'$ and the HICP weight year $y$
may therefore differ. To align the two sources, each HICP weight year $y$ is
matched with the most recent HBS wave available at or before that year. If no
earlier HBS wave is available, the earliest HBS wave is used.

As a rule, the HICP data is available on Eurostat at a level of detail
corresponding to the 5-digit COICOP classification. However, certain product
categories lack price data for specific periods. For instance, early-year
price data for medical consultation services ("062") is often unavailable. To
address these gaps, missing price indices are estimated based on the price
developments of their parent product categories. In the case of "062", the
price movements of the broader "health services" category ("06") are used to
estimate the missing data.

\subsection*{Price indices and notation}

Let $p_{j,y,m}$ denote the HICP price index of product category $j$ in month
$m$ of year $y$. Let $p_{j,y,0}$ denote the price reference used for the HICP
weight year $y$; in the chained annual calculation this corresponds to the
December price index of the previous year. Eurostat defines the HICP as a
chain-linked Laspeyres-type index: item indices are first unchained within the
weight year, aggregated with annual expenditure weights, and then chained
again.\footnote{See the \texttt{hicp} package vignette, which implements the
Eurostat HICP aggregation formulas:
\url{https://cran.r-project.org/web/packages/hicp/vignettes/hicp_intro.html}.}
The group-specific within-year price index therefore combines unchained product
indices with the group-specific weights defined above:

\begin{equation}
P_{g,y,m}
=
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{J}_y}
\omega_{j,g,y}
\cdot
\frac{p_{j,y,m}}{p_{j,y,0}}.
\end{equation}

The chained price index for household group $g$, denoted $I_{g,y,m}$, is then
obtained by linking each within-year index to the previous December:

\begin{equation}
I_{g,y,m}
=
I_{g,y-1,12}
\cdot
P_{g,y,m}.
\end{equation}

This notation keeps the two objects distinct: $\omega_{j,g,y}$ is the annual
group-specific HICP weight of product $j$, while $I_{g,y,m}$ is the resulting
monthly chained price index for group $g$.

\subsection*{Inflation and inflation gap}


Inflation in a given month for each household group $g$ results from the sum
of product-level contributions, defined in
Appendix~\ref{sec:app-contributions}:

\begin{equation} 
\pi_{g,y,m}
=
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{J}_y}
\text{Contrib}_{j,g,y,m}.
\end{equation} 


The inflation gap between two household groups $g_1$ and $g_2$ is defined as:

\begin{equation} 
\Delta\pi_{g_1,g_2,y,m}
=
\pi_{g_1,y,m}
-
\pi_{g_2,y,m}.
\end{equation}

For income quintiles, the inter-quintile inflation gap is:

\begin{equation} 
\text{IQR}_{y,m}
=
\pi_{Q1,y,m}
-
\pi_{Q5,y,m}.
\end{equation} \\

%In one hand,at household category level (and not at the individual level) we ignore intra-group heterogeneity. On the other hand, this approach flully covers the household consumption basket and allows for cross-country comparisons. 

%ICompared to scanner data (Appendix A) which are available only for supermarket products, the inflation gap offers at least three key advantages. First, it encompasses the entire household consumption basket. Second, it can be updated monthly to compute recent developments. Third, it is comparable across euro area countries.

The inflation gap, a measure of inflation inequality, offers several advantages over using scanner data (Appendix A). First, the inflation gap encompasses the entire household consumption basket, unlike scanner data, which is limited to supermarket products. This broader scope is essential to remain consistent with the basket of the HICP. Second, the inflation gap can be updated monthly, providing timely insights into recent inflation developments to monitor the distributional effects of price changes. Third, the inflation gap is comparable across euro area countries, facilitating analysis of inflation inequality patterns and policy effectiveness within a monetary union.

 However, the inflation gap does not account for changes in household behavior in response to rising prices. Wealthier households have greater flexibility to mitigate the effects of inflation by reducing discretionary spending, such as on leisure activities, or by substituting with lower-cost or lower-quality goods and services. \footnote{Recent household surveys conducted in France have explored how households adapt their consumption habits to inflation. These surveys show that lower-income households are more likely than higher-income households to report changes in their consumption patterns in response to rising prices (Credoc (2024), Insee (2024))}

Variations in consumption patterns between high- and low-income groups significantly influence the extent to which each group is affected by price changes within a specific consumption category. When the share of consumption for a particular good is substantially higher among lower-income households compared to higher-income households, price increases for that good disproportionately impact poorer households. Conversely, when both lower- and higher-income groups allocate similar proportions of their income to a given good, price changes tend to affect all households uniformly, thereby reducing disparities in inflation's impact and mitigating overall inflation inequality.

\subsection{Inflation Burden}

Inflation gaps measure differences in the growth rate of household-group price
indices, but they do not directly express the cost of inflation relative to
household resources. To complement the group-specific inflation rate
$\pi_{g,y,m}$ defined above, we compute an inflation burden indicator by scaling
inflation by the consumption-to-income ratio of group $g$. Let $c^Y_g$ denote
consumption as a percentage of disposable income in the latest annual source
year $Y$ available before year $y$. The burden is defined as:
\[
B_{g,y,m} = \pi_{g,y,m}\frac{c^Y_g}{100},
\]
and is expressed in percentage points of disposable income. Hence, two groups
with similar inflation rates may face different burdens when their propensities
to consume out of income differ. The figure reports this indicator for France
by income quintile, using level-3 consumption baskets.

\section{Simulations}


\subsection{Heterogeneous Effects of Shocks}

Recent inflation episodes have made clear that aggregate price indices can mask
substantial heterogeneity in the inflation shocks faced by households. The
standard decomposition attributes inflation inequality to differences in
expenditure shares across broad consumption categories: low-income households
spend more on necessities such as food, energy, and rents, and are therefore
more exposed when these relative prices rise. Sangani (2026) documents that
inflation shocks may also be heterogeneous within narrowly defined product
categories, because households at different points of the income distribution
purchase different varieties of the same goods.

Following the input-output price approach, we trace sectoral cost shocks through
production networks and map their incidence onto household consumption baskets.
Unlike aggregate approaches such as Corsello and Riggi (2026), this exercise
does not identify dynamic macroeconomic responses, but it delivers a
decomposition of the direct and indirect components of cost pass-through. We map
$\Delta p=(I-A')^{-1}s$ to consumption categories using the NACE--COICOP bridge
and aggregate with group-specific expenditure weights. The simulated inflation
effect for group $g$ is $w_g'\Delta p$, and inflation inequality is measured as
the difference between group-specific effects, for example
$(w_{Q1}-w_{Q5})'\Delta p$ for the income gap.

\subsection{Effects of Unconventional Fiscal Policies in the 2021-2023 Inflation Episode}

This section evaluates the distributional impact of unconventional fiscal policies on inflation inequality, with a particular focus on targeted interventions such as energy price shields and VAT reductions in France and Spain.

In response to the energy crisis, euro area governments introduced a broad array of measures to cushion households from surging energy costs (Sgaravatti et al., 2021). These included both direct income support—such as cash transfers to vulnerable groups—and price-based interventions, including temporary VAT reductions and price ceilings on gas, electricity, and fuel. While the latter are not explicitly targeted at lower-income households, they can still reduce inflation inequality by moderating the price increases of essential energy products.

In France, income-based instruments such as the 'chèque énergie' were specifically designed to support households with below-median incomes. Abdnoui et al. (2023) show that these measures largely benefited the most vulnerable households. In parallel, France and Spain implemented broader market interventions—including energy price caps and VAT cuts—that contributed to lowering average inflation and compressing the inflation gap across income groups.

To quantify the effect of these fiscal measures on inflation inequality, we construct a counterfactual scenario in which no such policies were enacted. Specifically, we compare the actual trajectory of retail gas and electricity prices with the estimated evolution of market prices in the absence of intervention. This comparison allows us to isolate the extent to which fiscal policy contributed to reducing inflationary pressures and their unequal distribution across households.

\section{Conclusion}


%This paper a set of public indicators to measure inflation inequalities in real-time monthly across socio-demographic groups in the Euro Area.

This paper presents a methodology to build easily implementable inflation inequality indicators in the euro area. The approach presented in this paper involves indicators based on the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and household budget surveys. 

%results
Our results show that inflation inequality across income groups in the euro area has remained relatively contained during the recent inflationary episode, especially in large countries such as Germany, France, and Spain. We also document strong cross-country heterogeneity in inflation inequality, particularly in countries dependent on natural gas imports, such as the Baltic states. 

Inflation inequalities by age and residence are more pronounced, with older and rural households facing consistently higher inflation due to their larger share of spending on essential goods like food and energy. We also find that unconventional fiscal measures, including VAT cuts and energy price shields, have significantly mitigated inflation inequality in countries like France and Spain, especially among lower-income households.

To improve accuracy, it is essential to compute inflation inequality indicators at the most granular level available within the consumption product classification. Recent advances in data availability, such as supermarket scanner data at the bar-code level or bank transaction records, provide new opportunities to improve the precision of inflation and consumption measurement.

\section{Italy HBS inputs}
\label{sec:app-italy-hbs}

Italy requires a specific treatment for some household-group HBS inputs. The
Eurostat harmonised HBS table reports income-quintile consumption baskets for
Italy in 2005, but not complete income-quintile baskets for the 2015 and 2020
waves. We therefore construct estimated 2015 and 2020 income-quintile baskets
with a latent income-score model. The 2005 Eurostat income-quintile baskets are
used only as an aggregate calibration target; the household-level 2015 and 2020
Istat HBS public-use files provide the characteristics and expenditure patterns
used to form the target-year groups.

For age groups, the Italian Istat public-use HBS files for 2015 and 2020 are
read directly from the original files, using the ECOICOP v1 expenditure
aggregates available in the microdata. These files provide direct division-level
expenditure variables, which are used to build age-group HBS inputs by the age
of the household reference person. For residence-area groups, the Istat
public-use files used here do not expose the Eurostat degree-of-urbanisation
classification. We therefore leave the residence-area totals blank in this
appendix table rather than infer them from basket shares or from an imperfect
housing-type proxy.

For each household $i$ in target year $y$, let $X_{i,y}$ denote observed
characteristics such as age and education of the reference person,
labour-market status, occupation, housing tenure, housing surface, ownership of
durable goods, internet access, subjective economic resources and situation,
the absolute-poverty indicator, and household consumption level. A latent
socio-economic score is defined as:

\begin{equation}
z_{i,y} = X_{i,y}\beta_y .
\end{equation}

Let $r_{i,y}(\beta_y)$ be the weighted rank of household $i$ in the target-year
distribution of this score. The probability that household $i$ belongs to
latent income group $g \in \{1,\ldots,5\}$ is:

\begin{equation}
p_{i,g,y}(\beta_y)
=
\frac{
\exp\{-\tau [r_{i,y}(\beta_y)-m_g]^2\}
}{
\sum_{h=1}^{5}\exp\{-\tau [r_{i,y}(\beta_y)-m_h]^2\}
},
\end{equation}

where $m_g \in \{0.1,0.3,0.5,0.7,0.9\}$ is the centre of quintile $g$ and
$\tau$ governs the sharpness of the probabilistic assignment. This soft
assignment avoids forcing households close to a quintile boundary into a single
group with probability one.

For COICOP division $j$, the probabilistic expenditure share of group $g$ in
target year $y$ is:

\begin{equation}
\widehat s_{j,g,y}(\beta_y)
=
\frac{
\sum_i w_{i,y} p_{i,g,y}(\beta_y) e_{i,j,y}
}{
\sum_k \sum_i w_{i,y} p_{i,g,y}(\beta_y) e_{i,k,y}
},
\end{equation}

where $w_{i,y}$ is the household survey weight and $e_{i,j,y}$ is household
expenditure on product division $j$. The score parameters are calibrated at the
aggregate level by matching the target-year probabilistic baskets to the
observed 2005 Eurostat income-quintile gradient:

\begin{equation}
\widehat\beta_y
=
\arg\min_{\beta_y}
\sum_{g=1}^{5}\sum_j
\left[
\widehat s_{j,g,y}(\beta_y)
-
s^{Eurostat}_{j,g,2005}
\right]^2
+ \lambda \|\beta_y\|^2 .
\end{equation}

This method uses individual and household characteristics in the 2015 and 2020
Italian HBS files, but the calibration remains aggregate because the 2005
Italian HBS microdata are not available in the workflow. The resulting groups
should therefore be interpreted as latent income groups rather than observed
income quintiles. The score model allows changes in the composition of
households and their observed expenditure patterns in 2015 and 2020 to affect
the estimated level-2 COICOP baskets, while anchoring the broad income gradient
on the observed 2005 income-quintile HBS structure.

Table~\ref{tab:it-hbs-all-products} reports the aggregate all-products
consumption shares used to document the available Italian HBS inputs. For age
groups, the entries are true survey-weighted shares of annual household
consumption in the target year, computed as group consumption divided by total
household consumption. For income groups, the 2015 and 2020 groups are latent
rather than directly observed survey groups; their aggregate shares are
therefore kept anchored to the 2005 Eurostat income-quintile distribution. This
normalisation fixes the size of the latent income groups in the descriptive
table, but it does not freeze their baskets: the level-2 COICOP composition used
by the inflation calculations can vary across HBS waves and across latent
groups. Table~\ref{tab:it-hbs-latent-income-coefficients} reports the
coefficients of the latent income-score model used to construct these
target-year baskets.

\section{Contribution to inflation}
\label{sec:app-contributions}

The contribution of product $j$ to inflation for household group $g$ is
computed using the product price movement and the group-specific product
weight. Because the price index is chained annually, the contribution in month
$m$ of year $y$ uses the relevant current and previous HICP weight years:

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\text{Contrib}_{j,g,y,m}
=
\frac{I_{g,y-1,12}}{I_{g,y-1,m}}
\times
\omega_{j,g,y-1}
\times
\left(
\frac{p_{j,y,m} - p_{j,y-1,12}}{p_{j,y-1,12}}
\right)
+ \\
\frac{I_{g,y-2,12}}{I_{g,y-1,m}}
\times
\omega_{j,g,y-2}
\times
\left(
\frac{p_{j,y-1,12} - p_{j,y-1,m}}{p_{j,y-2,12}}
\right).
\end{split}
\end{equation}

where $p_{j,y,m}$ is the price index of product $j$ in month $m$ of year $y$,
$I_{g,y,m}$ is the chained price index for group $g$, and
$\omega_{j,g,y}$ is the group-specific HICP weight of product $j$ in weight
year $y$.

This approach keeps product-level contributions consistent with the chained
inflation measures used for each household group. It is therefore useful for
interpreting which product categories drive the inflation rate and the
inflation gap between groups.

\section{Validation Tests}

This appendix reports validation checks for the inflation series recalculated with the package methodology. The objective is not to replicate the official aggregate index mechanically: household-group indices are built by combining official item prices and weights with group-specific expenditure shares, and the group average is then compared with the official all-items series. The validation is therefore a diagnostic on the size and time profile of the residual difference between the package recalculation and the published aggregate.

Figure~\ref{fig:fr-validation-rate} compares the French year-on-year inflation rate recomputed with detailed item prices and household-group expenditure shares to the published all-items HICP. The grey bars report the monthly difference between the package recalculation and the published all-items HICP, in percentage points.

Figure~\ref{fig:fr-validation-level} performs a complementary validation in index levels. The package first constructs chained price indices for French income groups and then compares the average across household groups with the published all-items HICP rebased to the same base year. This level comparison is useful because small monthly rate differences can accumulate in long-run index measures.

Finally, Table~\ref{tab:fr-decile-validation} compares the package's annual inflation rates by French standard-of-living decile with the official annual INSEE decile series. The reported offsets remain small, although they should be read with the same caveat: the package combines detailed prices and expenditure structures rather than reproducing the exact INSEE production system.
