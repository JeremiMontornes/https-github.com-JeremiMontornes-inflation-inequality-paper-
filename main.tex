\begin{abstract}
This article examines the heterogeneous effects of inflation across household categories in the euro area. Differences in consumption patterns between households and inflation dispersion across products have generated inflation inequalities. The inflationary episode, driven by rising imported prices, has mainly affected households who spend a larger share of their income on energy and food. In large countries of the euro area, inflation differentials between income quintiles have been low over the period, but differentials by age and residence area have been more pronounced. The tariff shields have significantly contributed to reducing inflation inequalities.


\vspace{1em} 

\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Inflation, Distribution of Income, Unconventional Fiscal Policy\\
\noindent \textbf{JEL codes:} E31, D30, C43 

\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}


This study examines the effect of the recent inflation surge on euro area households. Since mid-2021, inflation rates in the euro area rose sharply, displaying considerable dispersion across countries and socio-demographic groups. Central banks target aggregate inflation, but the welfare cost of a given aggregate inflation rate is not necessarily uniform across households. High inflation disproportionately affects households whose consumption baskets are more exposed to rapidly rising prices, in particular households with higher expenditure shares on energy and food. The 2021--2023 episode was not only an aggregate inflation shock, but also a large relative-price shock, initially driven by imported energy prices. Its incidence therefore depended on household exposure to energy-intensive items, including heating and transport.

%The consumer price index (CPI) typically reflects an average rate of price change for an average household. However, households consume different baskets of goods, leading to wide variations around the average. The main objective of this paper is to document how households face heterogeneous inflation because of the differences in their consumption patterns, and to measure the effects of unconventional fiscal policies decided in 2022-2023.


The literature identifies three channels through which inflation produces heterogeneous effects: wealth, income, and consumption. Inflation negatively impacts wealth by eroding the real value of nominal assets \citep{doepke2006inflation}. Conversely, it benefits indebted households, particularly those with fixed interest rate loans, as the real value of their debt diminishes. Moreover, inflation reduces the purchasing power of income, especially for households with incomes and savings not fully adjusted to inflation. Base wages, which often do not fully adjust to inflation, remain unchanged for the typical one-year wage contract period. Recent work has also paid renewed attention to inflation inequality through the consumption channel, since households buy different baskets of goods and services and are therefore exposed differently when relative prices change \citep{jaravel2021inflation,cravino2020household,argente2021cost}. This consumption channel can be measured using category-level inflation rates for household groups \citep{hobijn2005inflation}, or with more granular household-level expenditure and price data \citep{kaplan2017inflation}. %Recent studies, such as \cite{cardoso2022heterogeneous} and \cite{pallotti2024bears}, have sought to estimate these three channels simultaneously. Their analysis leverages newly available granular bank account data, which offer detailed insights into household financial positions and variations in consumption structures.

This work builds on studies by \cite{bobasu2023impact} and \cite{claeys2022inflation} that analyzed inflation inequality at euro area level, with slightly differing methodologies and results. \cite{bobasu2023impact} estimates inter-quintile inflation gaps of approximately 0.8 percentage points in September 2022, which narrowed to zero by early 2023 for the euro area, using 2015 household budget survey weights. \cite{claeys2022inflation}  findings align with our results. However, the methodology exclusively uses HBS weights, without adjusting the weights based on HBS-corrected price indices for quintile-specific inflation (Q1 and Q5). This adjustment leads to slight discrepancies between their overall inflation estimates and the harmonized price indices of the respective countries. 

We calculate inflation rates experienced by households across different categories within each euro area country, as well as for the euro area as a whole. The objective is to provide a replicable methodology for estimating inflation by income group, age group, and area of residence using harmonized and publicly available datasets. Inflation rates by categories are first computed at the country level and then aggregated for the euro area following closely the Euostat's HICP methodology. These indicators can be updated at monthly frequency, extended to more granular HBS national datasets. Additionally, inflation inequalities have been affected by the effect of unconventional fiscal policies implemented in 2022-2023  tariff shield policies and temporary VAT cuts across euro area countries [...])

inflation shocks may also be heterogeneous within narrowly defined product

This paper makes two contributions to the renewed interest in inflation inequality. First, it provides a publicly available and easily updatable dataset constructed using harmonized country-level inflation rates by household categories, ensuring consistency with the official Harmonised Index of Consumer Prices (HICP). Second, it examines the effect of unconventional fiscal policies, with a particular focus on the mitigating impacts of tariff shield policies and temporary VAT cuts implemented across euro area countries. Understanding the heterogeneous effects of inflation is important for assessing the effectiveness of policy responses during the 2022 energy crisis, because measures that reduce average inflation may have different distributional effects depending on which prices they affect.

Additionally, the study assesses the effect of unconventional fiscal policies implemented in 2022-2023, highlighting the mitigating effects of tariff shield policies and temporary VAT cuts across euro area countries.

Our main results are the following. Inflation inequality exhibits substantial variation across euro area countries. Within large economies, such as Germany, Spain, and France, inflation inequality remains relatively low since 2021. An exception is Italy, where disparities became pronounced during the energy price surge. In large countries, the inflation gap between the first and the fifth income quintiles is close to zero, partly because the product contributions to the gap tend to offset one another and because fiscal measures compressed some energy price increases. However, more significant differences emerge when considering demographic factors such as age and geographical factors, particularly between urban and rural households. Rural households face higher energy inflation in several countries, reflecting greater reliance on private transport, longer travel distances, and fewer short-run alternatives to fuel consumption.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. The first section describes the data and the methodology. The second section examines the developments of inflation inequality in the euro area. The third section presents simulation exercises about the hetergenous effects of unconventional fiscal policies.

\section{Data and Methodology}

This section introduces the data sources and methodological framework employed in the analysis to build the inflation inequality indicators.
% Moreover, we provide an open-access package that enables users to replicate and update our indicators.

\subsection{Data}

%The aim of calculating inflation  by household category is to construct inflation  that takes account of differences in household consumption structure. The data includes HICP by country, HBS weights (with a discussion on harmonization), and country weights based on country consumption.


Headline inflation represents the spending patterns of an average household. Estimating inflation for specific household categories requires the use of Household Budget Surveys (HBS), which are national surveys on consumer expenditure. These surveys offer detailed insights into household characteristics, including income levels, the age of the household reference person, and the area of residence.

\subsection*{Consumer Price Indices}

This analysis is based on publicly available data, primarily the Harmonised Index of Consumer Prices (HICP) constructed by national statistical institutes in collaboration with Eurostat. 

The HICP offers a standardized measure of inflation, ensuring comparability among member states. Eurostat aggregates national HICPs. Each country's HICP is based on price changes for a fixed basket of goods and services, weighted according to household expenditure from country-specific sources (national accounts, HBS, etc.). These indices are then combined into the aggregate HICP using country-specific weights that reflect their share of total  household consumption expenditure. The calculation ensures comparability by adhering tothe Regulation EU 2016/796 and consistent data collection practices. The HICP is compiled according to the classificication of houshold expenditures ECOICOP ver. 2.

To ensure the HICP accurately represents current consumption patterns, Eurostat updates its weighting structure annually. The updated weights are implemented with the January release of the HICP each year and remain fixed throughout the calendar year. This practice ensures consistency in the measurement of price changes over the year. This approach aligns with the Laspeyres index formula, which uses fixed base-period weights to measure price changes over time. 
Appendix~\ref{app:substitution-bias} reports a simple diagnostic of the potential upper-level substitution bias implied by this fixed-weight logic.

Figure~\ref{fig:hicp} illustrates the developments across different HICP components in the euro area since 2014. The HICP components show significant volatility in energy and fuel prices. The sharp rise starting around mid-2021, peaking in 2022, is due to post-COVID recovery, supply chain disruptions, and geopolitical tensions. After the peaks in 2022, there is a visible correction across most components. Energy prices dropped sharply, while food prices and other components showed more gradual stabilization.

\subsection*{Household Budget Survey} 


The heterogeneity of consumption patterns among households is a well-documented pattern (e.g. "Engel's laws"). However, the accurate measurement of household-level consumption continues to present significant challenges. 

The Household Budget Surveys (HBS) are conducted on a large sample of households in each euro area country. Households are required to document their expenditures on consumer goods and services over a designated period, typically two weeks. These surveys capture the values of expenditures across COICOP categories.

HBS data are generally collected every five years. Countries like Italy or Spain have yearly HBS surveys. The most recent and complete HBS wave generally took place in 2020. The 2020 HBS wave was actually conducted between 2018 and 2022 in most EU Member States, while data for Cyprus, Malta, and France were gathered earlier, between 2015 and 2017.\footnote{The following HBS wave is the 2025 wave, with increased harmonization between countries.}

However, using Spain as an example, Figure~\ref{fig:app-es-income-coicop-weights} suggests that, while behavioural changes over the course of a couple of years were significant, the percentage-point differences in the weights of the main consumption categories between low- and high-income households remained relatively stable. This is the dimension that matters most for our estimates. Despite changes in overall consumption patterns, the impact of recent behavioural changes on inflation inequality may therefore be limited, since consumption shares often move in the same direction for different income groups. If this pattern also holds in other countries, using the same expenditure weights over short periods is unlikely to be a major source of bias.

\subsection{Methodology}

%We explain the harmonized methodology applied across all euro area countries, utilizing an R package. The average of inflation measures by categories equals average inflation (cf. Jaravel?). This section presents the weighting methodology used to combine HBS expenditure patterns  with official HICP weights while ensuring consistency with the published aggregation.

\subsection*{Weights}

Let $\omega^{HICP}_{j,y}$ denote the official annual HICP weight of product
category $j$ in weight year $y$. Let $g$ denote a household group, such as an
income quintile, an age group, or a residence-area group. The HBS correction
factor is defined as:

\begin{equation}
c^{HBS}_{j,g,y'}
=
\frac{E^{HBS}_{j,g,y'}}{E^{HBS}_{j,\mathrm{all},y'}},
\end{equation}

where $E^{HBS}_{j,g,y'}$ is HBS expenditure on product category $j$ by group
$g$ in HBS wave $y'$, and $E^{HBS}_{j,\mathrm{all},y'}$ is the corresponding
expenditure for all households. $E^{HBS}_{g,y'}$ and
$E^{HBS}_{\mathrm{all},y'}$ denote total HBS consumption expenditure by group
$g$ and by all households, respectively. The coefficient $c^{HBS}_{j,g,y'}$
therefore measures the share of aggregate HBS expenditure on product $j$
accounted for by group $g$. In the paper,
we use the RAS calibration described below.

%The relative expenditures weihted  is reported
%in Appendix~\ref{sec:app-weight-normalization}.

The baseline weights, denoted $\omega_{j,g,y}$ in the formulas below, are
obtained from the RAS calibration implemented in the package function
\texttt{calculate\_}\allowbreak\texttt{weights()}. The algorithm preserves the
relative HBS expenditure profiles while making the expenditure-weighted average
of group baskets reproduce the official HICP item weights. Appendix
\ref{sec:app-weight-normalization} describes the RAS algorithm and compares it
with the simpler relative-expenditure normalization.

The Household Budget Survey is conducted only once every five years. In
contrast, the expenditure weights used in the construction of the HICP are
updated annually. The year of the HBS data $y'$ and the HICP weight year $y$
may therefore differ. To align the two sources, each HICP weight year $y$ is
matched with the most recent HBS wave available at or before that year. If no
earlier HBS wave is available, the earliest HBS wave is used.

As a rule, the HICP data is available on Eurostat at a level of detail
corresponding to the 5-digit COICOP classification. However, certain product
categories lack price data for specific periods. For instance, early-year
price data for medical consultation services ("062") is often unavailable. To
address these gaps, missing price indices are estimated based on the price
developments of their parent product categories. In the case of "062", the
price movements of the broader "health services" category ("06") are used to
estimate the missing data.

\subsection*{Group-level Consumer Price Indices}

Let $p_{j,y,m}$ denote the HICP price index of product category $j$ in month
$m$ of year $y$. Let $p_{j,y,0}$ denote the price reference used for the HICP
weight year $y$; in the chained annual calculation this corresponds to the
December price index of the previous year. Eurostat defines the HICP as a
chain-linked Laspeyres-type index: item indices are first unchained within the
weight year, aggregated with annual expenditure weights, and then chained
again.\footnote{See the \texttt{hicp} package vignette, which implements the
Eurostat HICP aggregation formulas:
\url{https://cran.r-project.org/web/packages/hicp/vignettes/hicp_intro.html}.}
The group-specific within-year price index therefore combines unchained product
indices with the group-specific weights defined above:

\begin{equation}
P_{g,y,m}
=
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{J}_y}
\omega_{j,g,y}
\cdot
\frac{p_{j,y,m}}{p_{j,y,0}}.
\end{equation}

The chained price index for household group $g$, denoted $I_{g,y,m}$, is then
obtained by linking each within-year index to the previous December:

\begin{equation}
I_{g,y,m}
=
I_{g,y-1,12}
\cdot
P_{g,y,m}.
\end{equation}

This notation keeps the two objects distinct: $\omega_{j,g,y}$ is the annual
group-specific HICP weight of product $j$, while $I_{g,y,m}$ is the resulting
monthly chained price index for group $g$.

quintiles at the euro-area aggregate level. This suggests that, once country
aggregation is taken into account, the recent inflation surge was primarily a
broad-based price-level shock rather than a strong source of income-based
inflation inequality for the euro area as a whole.

This pattern contrasts with the larger long-run gaps documented for the United
States in \cite{jaravel2024distributional}. Several differences may account for
this comparison. First, the euro-area 
reported here aggregate national indices and therefore average across countries
with different price dynamics and fiscal responses. The smaller long-run
income gap in Figure~\ref{fig:ea20_long_run_income} should therefore be read as
abroad-basket, cross-country comparable inflation inequality,
not as evidence that within-product or household-level heterogeneity is absent.

Variations in consumption patterns between high- and low-income groups significantly influence the extent to which each group is affected by price changes within a specific consumption category. To complement the group-specific inflation rate
$\pi_{g,y,m}$, derived from the chained group price index $I_{g,y,m}$, we
compute an inflation burden indicator by scaling inflation by the
consumption-to-income ratio of group $g$. To avoid confusing this object with
the HBS correction factor $c^{HBS}_{j,g,y'}$ used in the construction of
weights, let $\kappa_{g,y'}$ denote consumption expenditure as a percentage
of disposable income for group $g$ in the HBS or income-source year $y'$
matched to price year $y$. The burden is defined as:
\begin{equation}
b_{g,y,m} = \pi_{g,y,m}\frac{\kappa_{g,y'}}{100},
\end{equation}
and is expressed in percentage points of disposable income. Hence, two groups
with similar inflation rates may face different burdens when their propensities

interpretation of policy incidence. Higher-income households generally have
more scope to cushion a price shock by reducing discretionary expenditure or
substituting towards cheaper varieties, whereas lower-income households devote
a larger share of expenditure to essential goods. Figure~\ref{fig:app-ea-essentials-discretionary}
in the appendix documents this difference in the composition of euro-area consumption baskets.
Figure~\ref{fig:es-inflation-burden} reports this indicator for Spain. The first quintile faces a systematically larger burden than the fifth quintile during the inflationary episode, reflecting both higher inflation exposure and a higher ratio of consumption to disposable income.

The inflation gap offers several advantages over scanner data for this type of comparison. First, it encompasses the entire household consumption basket, whereas scanner data are generally limited to supermarket products. This broader scope is essential to remain consistent with the basket of the HICP. Second, the inflation gap can be updated monthly, providing timely evidence on the distributional effects of price changes. Third, the indicator is comparable across euro area countries, facilitating cross-country analysis of inflation inequality and policy effectiveness. Appendix~\ref{app:substitution-bias} discusses a related aggregation-bias diagnostic.

The indicator also has limits. It measures changes in group-specific price indices for fixed or slowly updated consumption baskets, but it does not account for contemporaneous changes in household behavior in response to rising prices. Wealthier households may have greater flexibility to mitigate the effects of inflation by reducing discretionary spending, such as on leisure activities, or by substituting with lower-cost or lower-quality goods and services.\footnote{Recent literature on household responses to inflation in France shows that lower-income households are more likely than higher-income households to report changes in their consumption patterns in response to rising prices \citep{sangani2026cheapflation}.}

\subsection{Household-level inflation}

The group-level inflation is useful for comparability across countries, but it only captures the component of inflation inequality that arises between broad household categories, such as the first and fifth income quintiles. It can therefore hide substantial dispersion within the same group. To document this within-group heterogeneity, we use household-level HBS expenditure data and compute household-specific inflation rates. In the empirical exercise, we use the Spanish EPF, a publicly available HBS microdata source, and fix household baskets at their 2020 EPF composition. The HBS survey year is denoted $y'$ in the expenditure-share formulas below. For each household, expenditure shares are matched to monthly COICOP price indices from the HICP. Household-level inflation is then obtained from the household-specific price index implied by the household's own expenditure shares. The distributions below are weighted with the EPF survey weights.

We compute a fixed-basket Laspeyres logic at the household level.
Let $e^{HBS}_{i,j,y'}$ denote household $i$'s expenditure on COICOP item $j$ in
HBS wave $y'$, and let
\begin{equation}
\omega^{HBS}_{j,i,y'}
=
\frac{e^{HBS}_{i,j,y'}}{\sum_{k \in \mathcal{J}_{i,y'}} e^{HBS}_{i,k,y'}}
\end{equation}
be the corresponding household expenditure share, using the same convention as
the group-specific weights $\omega_{j,g,y}$ defined above. For household $i$,
the within-year price index in month $m$ of year $y$ is
\begin{equation}
P_{i,y,m}
=
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{J}_{i,y',y}}
\omega^{HBS}_{j,i,y'}
\frac{p_{j,y,m}}{p_{j,y,0}}
,
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{J}_{i,y',y}$ is the set of household expenditure items observed
in HBS wave $y'$ that can be matched to HICP prices in year $y$. In the package
implementation, households are passed to the same price-index routine as
household groups: the within-year Laspeyres movements are chain-linked by
household, and year-on-year monthly inflation rates $\pi_{i,y,m}$ are computed
from the resulting chained household price index. The annual household
inflation rate is the average over valid months,
\begin{equation}
\pi_{i,y}
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal{T}_{i,y}|}
\sum_{m \in \mathcal{T}_{i,y}}
\pi_{i,y,m}.
\end{equation}

%computationally demanding than the faster approximation that first aggregates
%item-level annual inflation and then applies household expenditure shares, but
%it is closer to the index construction used for the group-level indicators.

\section{Simulations}

This section evaluates the distributional impact of unconventional fiscal
policies on inflation inequality, with a particular focus on targeted
interventions such as energy price shields, VAT reductions, and fuel rebates in
France, Germany, Italy, and Spain.

\subsection{Effects of Unconventional Fiscal Policies in the 2021-2023 Inflation Episode}

In response to the energy crisis, euro area governments introduced a broad array of measures to cushion households from surging energy costs \citep{sgaravatti2021national,arregui2022targeted}. These included both direct income support, such as cash transfers to vulnerable groups, and price-based interventions, including temporary VAT reductions and price ceilings on gas, electricity, and fuel. While the latter are not explicitly targeted at lower-income households, they can still reduce inflation inequality by moderating the price increases of essential energy products. Cross-country evidence is consistent with this mechanism: countries with larger price-based support measures tended to display lower income-based inflation gaps during the energy crisis, whereas inflation inequality was more pronounced in countries where such measures were less extensive \citep{claeys2022inflation}.

The main measures differ across the four large economies considered in the simulations. France combined income-based instruments, such as the energy voucher, with gas and electricity tariff shields and a temporary fuel rebate. Spain relied on electricity tax and VAT cuts, a gas VAT reduction, the Iberian electricity-price mechanism, and a fuel rebate. Germany reduced VAT on gas and introduced gas and electricity price brakes, while Italy combined a gas VAT cut with system-charge reductions, fuel-tax measures, and targeted social energy bonuses. Table~\ref{tab:ea20-price-counterfactual-measures} in the appendix reports the corresponding euro-area inventory and indicates which measures are directly simulated in our price counterfactuals. Beyond France and Spain, we also construct counterfactual price indices for the countries and policy instruments for which the necessary statutory, price, and unit-conversion information is available; Appendix~\ref{sec:app-counterfactuals} reports these country-level counterfactual series.

To quantify the effect of these fiscal measures on inflation inequality, we construct a counterfactual scenario in which no such policies were enacted. Specifically, we compare the actual trajectory of retail gas and electricity prices with the estimated evolution of market prices in the absence of intervention. This comparison allows us to isolate the extent to which fiscal policy contributed to reducing inflationary pressures and their unequal distribution across households. These measures are temporary support instruments rather than substitutes for monetary policy: their direct effect operates through administered prices, tax-inclusive prices, or retail price ceilings, and is therefore concentrated in the product categories to which they apply.

\subsection{Methods}

We distinguish three cases. First, for regulated-price shields, we use official or external price series, such as the CRE information for France or the retail-wholesale electricity relationship for Spain, to recover the no-policy path. Second, for VAT and excise changes, we back out the reduced tax-inclusive price and reapply the normal rate. Third, for unit subsidies and fuel rebates, we add the statutory support amount back to reconstructed retail unit prices. 
%Fourth, for targeted social bonuses, we construct aggregate-equivalent tests when the information is sufficient, and otherwise flag the measure as pending when the statutory schedule or consumption denominator is missing. For Germany, Italy, Spain, and the remaining euro-area measures, the identification of these cases combines the policy inventory in \citet{arregui2022targeted} with the  dataset of \citet{sgaravatti2021national}.


Counterfactual price indices are constructed at the COICOP component level by replacing the observed policy-inclusive price path with a no-policy price path. Let $P_{j,t}$ denote the observed HICP component index for product $j$ in month $t$, and let $\widetilde P_{j,t}$ denote the corresponding counterfactual index. For each simulated measure, we recover a counterfactual retail price $\widetilde p_{j,t}$ and apply the implied price ratio to the observed component index:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde P_{j,t}
=
P_{j,t}
\frac{\widetilde p_{j,t}}{p_{j,t}} .
\end{equation}

\paragraph{VAT reductions.}
For VAT or excise-tax reductions, the observed retail price includes the reduced policy tax rate. The no-policy price is obtained by backing out the reduced tax-inclusive price and reapplying the normal tax rate:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde p_{j,t}
=
p_{j,t}
\frac{1+\tau^{0}_{j,t}}{1+\tau^{1}_{j,t}},
\end{equation}
where $\tau^{1}_{j,t}$ is the policy tax rate and $\tau^{0}_{j,t}$ is the no-policy tax rate.

\paragraph{Administered prices.}
For regulated or administered-price measures, such as electricity or gas tariff shields, the counterfactual price is taken from a no-policy reference path:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde p_{j,t}
=
p^{\mathrm{ref}}_{j,t}.
\end{equation}
For France, this reference path is built from CRE tariff information. If $T^{\mathrm{obs}}_{j,t}$ denotes the regulated tariff actually applied and $T^{\mathrm{ref}}_{j,t}$ the tariff that would have applied without the shield, the no-policy retail price is obtained by rescaling the observed price:
\begin{equation}
p^{\mathrm{ref}}_{j,t}
=
p_{j,t}
\frac{T^{\mathrm{ref}}_{j,t}}{T^{\mathrm{obs}}_{j,t}} .
\end{equation}
For electricity, the tariff information is taken from the CRE data and documentation on regulated electricity tariffs.\footnote{\url{https://www.cre.fr/}} For gas, we use the CRE reference-price series for residential consumers.\footnote{\url{https://www.cre.fr/}} For Spain, the electricity counterfactual is estimated from the pre-crisis relationship between retail electricity prices and wholesale market prices. Let $M_t$ denote the wholesale electricity price and let $D_t$ collect seasonal controls. The no-policy benchmark is obtained from
\begin{equation}
\log p^{\mathrm{ref}}_{j,t}
=
\widehat\alpha
+
\widehat\beta \log M^{\mathrm{ref}}_t
+
D_t'\widehat\gamma ,
\end{equation}
where the coefficients are estimated before the intervention period and $M^{\mathrm{ref}}_t$ is the wholesale price benchmark. Wholesale market data are taken from OMIE.\footnote{\url{https://www.omie.es/}}

\paragraph{Subsidies and rebates.}
For unit subsidies, fuel rebates, or per-kWh support schemes, the observed price is lower than the no-policy price by the statutory support amount. The counterfactual price is obtained by adding the support amount $s_{j,t}$ back to the observed unit price:
\begin{equation}
\widetilde p_{j,t}
=
p_{j,t}+s_{j,t}.
\end{equation}

The counterfactual component indices are then used in the same household-group inflation procedure as the observed indices. With group-specific expenditure weights $\omega_{j,g,t}$, the counterfactual inflation rate for group $g$ is
\begin{equation}
\widetilde \pi_{g,t}
=
\sum_j \omega_{j,g,t}
\Delta \log \widetilde P_{j,t},
\qquad
\Delta \pi_{g,t}^{\mathrm{policy}}
=
\widetilde \pi_{g,t}-\pi_{g,t}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Policy Options}

We estimate a simple household-level model of energy demand and compensating
needs. This framework is a simplified version of \cite{levell2026welfare}, who study
the welfare effects of energy price shocks and relief packages using rich
household panel data. Related policy evaluations include the comparison of fuel
price subsidies and transfers for France in \citet{bonnet2025compensating}, and
evidence on the distributional effects of the energy crisis and cost-of-living
measurement for the United Kingdom in \citet{levell2024distributional} and
\citet{martin2022revisiting}. Household $i$ consumes a vector of energy goods
$q_{ik}$, indexed by $k \in K$, and a composite numeraire good $y_i$, subject
to the budget constraint:

\begin{equation}
y_i + \sum_{k \in K} p_k q_{ik} = Y_i .
\end{equation}

Preferences are quasi-linear,
$U_i(y_i,\{q_{ik}\})=y_i+u_i(q_{i1},\ldots,q_{iK})$, so local demand
responses can be summarized by own- and cross-price elasticities:

\begin{equation}
d\log q_{ik}
=
\sum_{\ell \in K}
\varepsilon_{k\ell}
d\log p_{\ell}.
\end{equation}

A welfare benchmark can be expressed through compensating variation. Let
$e_i(p,u)$ denote the expenditure function, where $p$ is the full price vector
and $u$ a utility level. The compensating variation associated with a move from
$p^0$ to $p^1$ is:

\begin{equation}
CV_i
=
e_i(p^1,u_i^0)-e_i(p^0,u_i^0),
\end{equation}

so that, with $C_i=e_i(p^0,u_i^0)$,

\begin{equation}
\frac{CV_i}{C_i}
=
\frac{e_i(p^1,u_i^0)}{e_i(p^0,u_i^0)}-1 ,
\end{equation}

which is the household cost-of-living index minus one.

Estimating the exact COLI would require specifying household preferences and
substitution possibilities. We therefore rely on a first-order
expenditure-variation measure based on initial budget shares:

\begin{equation}
EV^{exp}_i
=
\sum_{k \in K}
s_{ik}
\Delta \log p_k,
\end{equation}

where $s_{ik}=p_k^0 q_{ik}^0/C_i$ is household $i$'s initial budget share for
energy good $k$. For small price changes, this fixed-basket measure approximates
the normalized compensating variation:

\begin{equation}
\frac{CV_i}{C_i}
\simeq
EV^{exp}_i
=
\sum_{k \in K}
s_{ik}
\Delta \log p_k .
\end{equation}

The approximation has a direct welfare interpretation under quasi-linear
preferences. Because the marginal utility of income is constant and income
effects are absent, compensating variation, equivalent variation, and changes in
Marshallian consumer surplus coincide for price changes in non-numeraire goods.
The fixed-basket expenditure variation can therefore be interpreted as the
first-order monetary welfare loss from the price shock, expressed as a share of
total household expenditure.

We implement the model in a reduced form by estimating own-price elasticities
for Spain. Spain is a natural first case because the EPF microdata are available
year by year and report both expenditure and quantities for electricity, gas,
and motor fuels. For household
$i$, energy good $k$, autonomous community $r$, and year $y$, the estimated
equation is:

\begin{equation}
\log q_{i,k,r,y}
=
\alpha_k
+
\varepsilon_k \log p_{i,k,r,y}
+
X_{i,y}'\beta_k
+
\mu_{r,k}
+
\lambda_{y,k}
+
u_{i,k,r,y},
\end{equation}

where $k$ denotes electricity, gas, or motor fuels. The variable $q_{i,k,r,y}$
is the reported quantity consumed, and $p_{i,k,r,y}$ is either the household
unit value, measured as expenditure divided by quantity, or a local price proxy
measured as the median unit value by autonomous community, year, and energy
good. The vector $X_{i,y}$ includes log total expenditure, log equivalised
income, and household size. The coefficient $\varepsilon_k$ is the estimated
price elasticity for energy good $k$. These estimates are intended as a
first-pass calibration of local demand responses for the Spanish policy
simulations, rather than as a fully structural demand system.

\input{tables/tab_ES_epf_energy_elasticity_estimates}

The estimates in Table~\ref{tab:es-epf-energy-elasticities} imply negative
price elasticities for all three energy goods. The household-unit-value
specification gives elasticities close to one in absolute value for electricity
and gas, and a larger response for motor fuels. The local-price-proxy
specification gives larger elasticities in absolute value, especially for gas
and motor fuels, reflecting the fact that identification comes from
cross-regional price variation in the currently available one-year sample. In
the policy simulations, we therefore treat the household-unit-value estimates
as the conservative benchmark and the local-price-proxy estimates as an upper

\section{Conclusion}


%This paper a set of public indicators to measure inflation inequalities in real-time monthly across socio-demographic groups in the Euro Area.

This paper presents a methodology to build timely inflation inequality indicators in the euro area based on the Harmonised Index of Consumer Prices (HICP) and household budget surveys. It provides a framework for monitoring how aggregate inflation translates into different household-group inflation rates across countries and over time.

%results
Our results show that inflation inequality across income groups in the euro area has remained contained during the recent inflationary episode, especially in large countries such as Germany, France, Italy, and Spain. We also document strong cross-country heterogeneity in inflation inequality, particularly in countries dependent on natural gas imports, such as the Baltic states. 

Inflation inequalities by age and residence are more pronounced, with older and rural households facing consistently higher inflation due to their larger share of spending on essential goods like food and energy. We also find that unconventional fiscal measures, including VAT cuts and energy price shields, have significantly mitigated inflation inequality in countries like France and Spain, especially among lower-income households. These measures operated directly on relative prices, particularly energy prices, and therefore reduced both average inflation and the inflation exposure of groups whose baskets were more energy intensive.

%Consumption-based inflation inequality captures only one dimension of the distributional effects of inflation. A complete assessment also requires accounting for income indexation, transfers, and wealth effects, which may dominate consumption-basket effects for some households \citep{cardoso2022heterogeneous,pallotti2024bears}.

Fiscal packages such as VAT reductions, fuel rebates, and tariff shields can have immediate effects on measured prices and can change relative prices across consumption categories. They can reduce the inflation burden of households whose expenditure is concentrated on regulated or subsidized items. At the same time, income breakdown is not sufficient to identify vulnerable households: exposure also depends on residence area, heating technology, mobility constraints, and the capacity to substitute away from energy-intensive consumption. By contrast, monetary policy affects the aggregate price level through broader transmission channels, with lags generally estimated at around 18 months. In the long run, monetary policy remains the instrument responsible for bringing inflation back to its target, including the 2 percent objective in the euro area, while fiscal policy is better understood as a temporary instrument for cushioning distributional effects during large relative-price shocks.

%limit
The group-level indicators remain subject to aggregation bias. To improve accuracy, it is essential to compute inflation inequality indicators at the most granular level available within the consumption product classification. Recent advances in data availability, such as supermarket scanner data at the bar-code level or bank transaction records, provide new opportunities to improve the precision of inflation and consumption measurement.

\section{Househlods Budget Surveys in Italy }
\label{sec:app-italy-hbs}

While Italy reports HBS data annually on the national statistical office's
website, the most recent Eurostat harmonised and complete HBS entry for Italy is 2005 . This
requires a specific treatment for some household-group data.  We therefore construct
estimated 2015 and 2020 age and income-quintile baskets.

 For age groups, thevItalian Istat public-use HBS files for 2015 and 2020 are read directly from the
original files, using the ECOICOP v1 expenditure aggregates available in the
microdata. These direct division-level expenditure variables are used to build
age-group HBS inputs by the age of the household reference person.

For income quintile, we first use the year in which
income-quintile and expenditure-quintile information can be compared to build a
2005 income/expenditure bridge by COICOP division and quintile, and then applying
this bridge to the target-year Istat HBS baskets by equivalised-expenditure
quintile before renormalising each corrected basket to 100 percent. These bridge-corrected income-quintile baskets are then  used as target-year
calibration targets for the latent income-score model, while the household-level
2015 and 2020 Istat HBS public-use files provide the characteristics and
expenditure patterns used to form the target-year groups.

For each household $i$ in target year $y$, let $X_{i,y}$ denote observed
characteristics such as age and education of the reference person,
labour-market status, occupation, housing tenure, housing surface, ownership of
durable goods, internet access, subjective economic resources and situation,
the absolute-poverty indicator, and household consumption level. A latent
socio-economic score is defined as:

\begin{equation}
z_{i,y} = X_{i,y}\beta_y .
\end{equation}

Let $r_{i,y}(\beta_y)$ be the weighted rank of household $i$ in the target-year
distribution of this score. The probability that household $i$ belongs to
latent income group $g \in \{1,\ldots,5\}$ is:

\begin{equation}
p_{i,g,y}(\beta_y)
=
\frac{
\exp\{-\tau [r_{i,y}(\beta_y)-m_g]^2\}
}{
\sum_{h=1}^{5}\exp\{-\tau [r_{i,y}(\beta_y)-m_h]^2\}
},
\end{equation}

where $m_g \in \{0.1,0.3,0.5,0.7,0.9\}$ is the centre of quintile $g$ and
$\tau$ governs the sharpness of the probabilistic assignment. This soft
assignment avoids forcing households close to a quintile boundary into a single
group with probability one.

For COICOP division $j$, the probabilistic expenditure share of group $g$ in
target year $y$ is:

\begin{equation}
\widehat s_{j,g,y}(\beta_y)
=
\frac{
\sum_i w_{i,y} p_{i,g,y}(\beta_y) x_{i,j,y}
}{
\sum_k \sum_i w_{i,y} p_{i,g,y}(\beta_y) x_{i,k,y}
},
\end{equation}

where $w_{i,y}$ is the household survey weight and $x_{i,j,y}$ is household
expenditure on product division $j$. The score parameters are calibrated at the
aggregate level by matching the target-year probabilistic baskets to the
bridge-corrected target-year income-quintile basket:

\begin{equation}
\widehat\beta_y
=
\arg\min_{\beta_y}
\sum_{g=1}^{5}\sum_j
\left[
\widehat s_{j,g,y}(\beta_y)
-
s^{bridge}_{j,g,y}
\right]^2
+ \lambda \|\beta_y\|^2 .
\end{equation}

%This method uses individual and household characteristics in the 2015 and 2020
I%talian HBS files, but the calibration remains aggregate because the target-year
%Italian public-use files do not report observed income quintiles. 
The resulting
groups should therefore be interpreted as latent income groups rather than
directly observed income quintiles. The bridge lets the broad income gradient
move with the target-year expenditure distribution, while retaining the 2005
evidence on how income-quintile and expenditure-quintile baskets differ.

Table~\ref{tab:it-hbs-all-products} reports the aggregate all-products
consumption shares used to document the available Italian HBS inputs. For age
groups, the entries are true survey-weighted shares of annual household
consumption in the target year, computed as group consumption divided by total
household consumption. For income groups, the 2015 and 2020 groups are latent
rather than directly observed survey groups; their aggregate shares are the
bridge-corrected target-year estimates. The resulting Italian distribution is
close to the pattern observed in neighbouring large euro-area countries: the
first quintile accounts for about 8 percent of total consumption, while the fifth
quintile accounts for about 39 percent, compared with roughly 9--10 percent and
33--36 percent in Germany, France and Spain. Table~\ref{tab:it-hbs-latent-income-coefficients}
reports the coefficients of the latent income-score model used to construct
these target-year baskets.

\section{Weighting}
\label{sec:app-weight-normalization}

We use the RAS (Row and Column Adjustment Scaling) algorithm.
RAS is a bi-proportional matrix scaling method that  alternately rescales rows and columns until the required
marginal totals are matched \citep{bacharach1965estimating}. In our setting,
we combine official HICP item weights with the relative HBS
expenditure profile of each household group.

Let $s_{g,y}$ denote the share of group $g$ in total consumption expenditure
used for HICP weight year $y$:
\begin{equation}
s_{g,y}
=
\frac{E^{HBS}_{g,y'}}{E^{HBS}_{\mathrm{all},y'}},
\qquad
\sum_g s_{g,y}=1,
\end{equation}
where $y'$ is the HBS wave matched to HICP weight year $y$. In the
implementation, $s_{g,y}$ is selected from the group consumption-share table
using the most recent HBS wave available at or before $y$; if no prior wave is
available, the earliest available wave is used. These targets are expenditure
shares, not population shares. For Italy, the income targets use the
Istat-based HBS inputs described in Appendix~\ref{sec:app-italy-hbs}.

Let $\mathcal{J}_y$ be the set of matched COICOP categories available in the
HICP weighting structure in year $y$. The RAS seed is
\begin{equation}
Z_{j,g,y}
=
\max\left\{
s_{g,y}\omega^{HICP}_{j,y}c^{HBS}_{j,g,y'},\epsilon
\right\},
\qquad
\epsilon=10^{-12}.
\end{equation}
The small positive floor is a numerical safeguard used in the package to keep
the RAS problem strictly non-negative after HICP-HBS matching. The calibrated
aggregate expenditure matrix $M_{j,g,y}$ is required to satisfy the original
HICP item-weight margins:
\begin{equation}
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{J}_y} M_{j,g,y}
=
s_{g,y}\sum_{j \in \mathcal{J}_y}\omega^{HICP}_{j,y}
\quad \text{for all } g,
\qquad
\sum_g M_{j,g,y}
=
\omega^{HICP}_{j,y}
\quad \text{for all } j.
\end{equation}

The RAS algorithm starts from $M^{(0)}_{j,g,y}=Z_{j,g,y}$ and repeats two
scaling steps. At iteration $t$, the row adjustment is
\begin{equation}
\widetilde{M}^{(t)}_{j,g,y}
=
M^{(t-1)}_{j,g,y}
\times
\frac{
s_{g,y}%\sum_{\ell \in \mathcal{J}_y}\omega^{HICP}_{\ell,y}
}{
\sum_{\ell \in \mathcal{J}_y} M^{(t-1)}_{\ell,g,y}
},
\end{equation}
and the column adjustment is
\begin{equation}
M^{(t)}_{j,g,y}
=
\widetilde{M}^{(t)}_{j,g,y}
\times
\frac{
\omega^{HICP}_{j,y}
}{
\sum_h \widetilde{M}^{(t)}_{j,h,y}
}.
\end{equation}
We iterats these two steps by weight year until the maximum absolute
row or column marginal error is below $10^{-10}$, with a maximum of 1,000
iterations.

The final group-specific HICP weights are obtained by dividing the calibrated
aggregate mass by the group expenditure share:
\begin{equation}
\omega_{j,g,y}
=
\frac{M_{j,g,y}}{s_{g,y}}.
\end{equation}
Thus the expenditure-share-weighted average of the group baskets reproduces
the official HICP item weights over the matched product set:
\begin{equation}
\sum_g s_{g,y}\omega_{j,g,y}
=
\omega^{HICP}_{j,y}.
\end{equation}
This preserves the relative information contained in the HBS expenditure
profiles while making the aggregate of the household-group baskets consistent
with the published HICP weighting structure, up to residual differences induced
by product coverage and COICOP matching.

\section{Heterogeneous Effects of Shocks}

Recent inflation episodes have made clear that aggregate price indices can mask
substantial heterogeneity in the inflation shocks faced by households. The
standard decomposition attributes inflation inequality to differences in
expenditure shares across broad consumption categories: low-income households
spend more on necessities such as food, energy, and rents, and are therefore
more exposed when these relative prices rise. 

Following the input-output price approach, we trace sectoral cost shocks through
production networks and map their incidence onto household consumption baskets.
Unlike aggregate approaches such as \cite{corsello2026inflation}, this exercise
does not identify dynamic macroeconomic responses, but it delivers a
decomposition of the direct and indirect components of cost pass-through. We map
$\Delta p=(I-A')^{-1}s$ to consumption categories using the NACE--COICOP bridge
and aggregate with group-specific expenditure weights. The simulated inflation
effect for group $g$ is $w_g'\Delta p$, and inflation inequality is measured as
the difference between group-specific effects, for example
$(w_{Q1}-w_{Q5})'\Delta p$ for the income gap.
