\section*{Input-output price shocks and inflation inequality}

Table~\ref{tab:shock-crude-oil-gas} reports the simulated effects of a 10 percent shock to crude oil and gas
prices. The average inflation effect ranges from 0.6 percentage point in Germany
and France to 1.1 percentage points in the Netherlands, with the euro area
average equal to 0.8 percentage point. Most of the effect is indirect, reflecting
the propagation of energy costs through production networks rather than only the
direct energy component in household consumption. Distributional effects are
small relative to the average effect. Income gaps are close to zero in the euro
area aggregate, while rural-urban gaps are more systematic, consistent with
greater exposure to transport and energy-intensive consumption among rural
households.

Table~\ref{tab:shock-cereals} considers a 10 percent shock to cereal prices. The average effect is of
a similar order of magnitude to the energy shock in several countries, reaching
about 1.2 percentage points in France, Italy, and Spain, and 1.0 percentage
point for the euro area aggregate. The indirect component is again quantitatively
important, indicating that cereal inputs affect final consumer prices through
processed food and downstream sectors. Distributional gaps remain limited in
most countries. Italy is the main exception in the income dimension, with a
positive Q1--Q5 gap, consistent with the larger food share in lower-income
household baskets.

Table~\ref{tab:shock-electronic-components} reports the effects of a 10 percent shock to electronic component
prices. The simulated aggregate inflation effects are much smaller, between 0.1
and 0.2 percentage point across the reported countries. Direct and indirect
components are both modest, and the distributional gaps by income, age, and
residence area are essentially zero after rounding. This result is consistent
with electronic components entering household consumption mainly through a
limited set of durable goods and through relatively diffuse production-chain
effects.
